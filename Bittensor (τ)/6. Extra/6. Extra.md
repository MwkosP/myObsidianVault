


# Bittensor — Extra (Q&A)

---

## Open Source Requirements

**Does a subnet have to be open source?** No. Subnet owners can keep their incentive mechanism code proprietary. However, most successful subnets are open source because miners need to understand what they're optimizing for. A black-box incentive mechanism makes it hard to attract good miners — they won't know what to build. Open source subnets tend to attract more competition and therefore produce better intelligence.

**Do miners have to be open source?** No. Miners can keep their model weights and architecture entirely private. The network only sees their outputs, not how they produce them. This is actually a feature — it encourages proprietary innovation. A miner with a genuinely better model has no obligation to reveal it.

**Do validators have to be open source?** No. Validators can keep their evaluation logic private. That said, if a validator's scoring methodology is opaque, miners can't calibrate their outputs to perform well, which hurts the subnet's quality. In practice, validators in well-run subnets publish their evaluation criteria.

**Is the core Bittensor protocol open source?** Yes. The core protocol (subtensor blockchain, bittensor Python library) is fully open source on GitHub under the MIT license. Anyone can audit it, fork it, or build on it.

## Subnet Questions

**Can anyone create a subnet?** Yes. Anyone who pays the TAO registration fee can create a subnet. No permission required. The fee scales with network demand — when many subnets exist, creating a new one costs more.

**Can a subnet be shut down?** Not by the protocol. Once created, a subnet runs as long as participants engage with it. The Opentensor Foundation cannot shut down individual subnets. However, a subnet can die naturally if it loses all miners and validators — no enforcement needed.

**Can a subnet owner change the incentive mechanism after launch?** Yes. Subnet owners can upgrade their incentive mechanism. This is both necessary (bugs, improvements) and risky (miners have built around the old rules). Major changes can cause participant exodus if not handled carefully.

**Can one entity own multiple subnets?** Yes. There is no limit on how many subnets one entity can create and own, as long as they pay the registration fee for each.

**What happens if a subnet owner disappears?** The subnet continues running. Miners and validators keep operating. Emissions keep flowing. The 18% owner share simply goes uncollected or accumulates. The subnet becomes ownerless but functional — like a company whose founder left but the factory still runs.

**Can subnets talk to each other?** Not natively at the protocol level yet. In theory, a miner in one subnet could use outputs from another subnet as inputs, but this is implemented at the application layer by the miner, not enforced by the protocol.

## Miner Questions

**Can a miner participate in multiple subnets?** Yes. A miner can register in as many subnets as they want, paying a registration fee per subnet. Many miners run the same model across multiple subnets or specialize different models per subnet.

**Can a miner be a validator at the same time?** Yes. The same entity can run both miner and validator nodes. However, validators cannot vote for their own miner UIDs — the protocol prevents direct self-dealing at the weight submission level.

**What happens to a miner's earnings if they go offline?** Their outputs stop. Validators who query them get no response or a timeout. Their weights drop. If they stay offline long enough, they fall to the bottom of the ranking and get pruned when a new miner wants to register.

**Can a miner game the incentive mechanism?** They can try. This is called "incentive hacking" — optimizing for the metric rather than the actual task. It's a known problem (Goodhart's Law). Well-designed subnets try to make the metric hard to game without actually solving the problem. Poorly designed subnets get farmed.

**Do miners need expensive hardware?** Depends entirely on the subnet. A text generation subnet on SN1 requires serious GPU compute to compete. A storage subnet (SN21) requires disk space. A prediction subnet might just need a good algorithm running on a CPU. Hardware requirements are subnet-specific.

**Can a miner see who is validating them?** Yes — validator UIDs are visible on-chain. But miners cannot selectively respond to validators. They respond to all queries.

## Validator Questions

**How much TAO do you need to be a validator?** It varies by subnet. Each subnet sets its own `min_stake` hyperparameter. Competitive subnets with high emissions require significant stake — potentially thousands of TAO — to have meaningful influence. Lower-traffic subnets have lower barriers.

**Can a validator refuse to score certain miners?** Technically yes — they can set `wᵢⱼ = 0` for any miner. But if they consistently ignore miners that the honest majority scores highly, their trust `T` drops and their emissions fall. Selective blanket-zeroing of good miners is penalized.

**What stops a validator from copy-pasting another validator's weights?** Nothing at the protocol level. "Weight copying" is a known attack — a lazy validator just mirrors a reputable validator's weights to earn emissions without doing evaluation work. Some subnets try to detect this through randomized query sets that should produce unique scores. It is an open problem.(Bonds have been made to tackle this)

**Can a validator operate across all subnets?** Yes. Validators register per-subnet but their stake is global. A well-resourced validator can run evaluation infrastructure across many subnets simultaneously.

## TAO & Economics Questions

**Can TAO be bought on exchanges?** Yes. TAO trades on major centralized and decentralized exchanges. It is not restricted to being earned — you can buy stake and become a validator or delegator without ever mining.

**Is TAO inflationary or deflationary?** Both, depending on the phase. Early in the network's life, emission rate exceeds burn rate — net inflationary. As the network matures, registration activity increases burn, halvings reduce emission, and net inflation decreases. Long-term it approaches deflationary as supply approaches 21M.

**Can you lose TAO by staking?** Not directly from staking — your stake doesn't get "slashed" like in Ethereum. However, if you delegate to a poor validator, you earn less yield. There is no protocol-level slashing mechanism in Bittensor (unlike Ethereum's PoS). Bad behavior is punished through reduced emissions, not stake destruction.

**Who received TAO at launch — was there a pre-mine?** There was a small initial allocation to the founding team and early contributors, but the vast majority of TAO supply is distributed through the emission schedule. The Opentensor Foundation has publicly committed to transparency on this. Unlike many crypto projects, Bittensor was not launched with a large VC pre-mine.

**What gives TAO value?** Fundamentally: the intelligence the network produces. TAO is needed to register subnets, register neurons, and stake as a validator. As more subnets produce useful intelligence and more people want to participate, demand for TAO increases against a fixed supply ceiling.

## Governance Questions

**Who controls Bittensor?** The Opentensor Foundation (OTF) currently acts as the steward of the protocol. However, the codebase is open source and the blockchain is decentralized. In practice, OTF has significant influence over protocol upgrades, but cannot unilaterally change the chain without node operators upgrading their software.

**Can the 21M supply cap be changed?** Technically it could be changed via a protocol upgrade if node operators accepted it. In practice this would be extremely controversial and likely cause a community split/fork. The cap is treated as sacrosanct, similar to Bitcoin's.

**Can subnet hyperparameters be changed without owner permission?** Some global parameters (like max number of subnets) are controlled at the protocol level and require a network-wide upgrade. Subnet-specific hyperparameters are controlled by the subnet owner. There is an evolving governance framework but it is less formalized than some other chains.

**Is there on-chain governance voting?** As of 2024, governance is relatively informal — the OTF makes protocol decisions with community input. Full on-chain governance (token-weighted voting on proposals) is not yet fully implemented but is part of the roadmap.

## Technical Questions

**What blockchain does Bittensor run on?** Bittensor runs on its own blockchain called **Subtensor**, built with Substrate (the same framework as Polkadot). It is not on Ethereum or any other existing chain.

**What language do you use to build on Bittensor?** The primary SDK is **bittensor** in Python. Subnet incentive mechanisms, miner logic, and validator logic are typically written in Python. The Subtensor blockchain itself is written in Rust.

**What is a "neuron" in Bittensor?** Neuron is the generic term for any registered participant — miners and validators are both neurons. Each neuron has a UID (unique ID) within a subnet, a hotkey (operational wallet), and a coldkey (secure storage wallet).

**What is the difference between a hotkey and a coldkey?**

- **Coldkey**: Secure wallet holding your TAO. Should be kept offline. Controls stake and funds.
- **Hotkey**: Operational key used for day-to-day network activity (submitting weights, responding to queries). Can be on a running server. Compromising a hotkey doesn't give access to your staked TAO.

**What is Subtensor?** Subtensor is the name of Bittensor's blockchain. It handles all on-chain state: stake balances, weight submissions, neuron registrations, emission distributions. The AI computation itself happens off-chain — only the scores and weights are recorded on-chain.

## Philosophical / Big Picture Questions

**Is Bittensor actually decentralized?** Partially. The blockchain layer is decentralized. But in practice, top validators hold a disproportionate share of stake, giving them significant influence over rankings and subnet scoring via SN0. This is a known tension — formal decentralization vs. practical power concentration.

**Can Bittensor actually compete with OpenAI?** The bet is not that Bittensor produces one model better than GPT-4. The bet is that a decentralized market for intelligence can aggregate and coordinate more diverse intelligence than any single lab — especially for specialized tasks. The comparison is less "one model vs one model" and more "market vs corporation."

**What happens when AI becomes a commodity?** If LLMs become cheap and commoditized, the incentive to mine text generation subnets may drop. Bittensor's design allows new subnets to emerge for whatever the next valuable form of intelligence is — the market adapts. The protocol is task-agnostic.

**Can Bittensor be regulated or shut down?** Individual subnet owners, miners, and validators can be targeted by regulators in their jurisdiction. The OTF can be pressured. But the underlying blockchain and protocol, once sufficiently decentralized, cannot be unilaterally shut down — similar to Bitcoin or Ethereum.
