


# Bittensor — Incentives

---

## Overview

The incentives system is the heart of Bittensor. It answers one question:

> **How do you distribute TAO to participants who genuinely contribute, while making it unprofitable to cheat?**

Everything — Yuma Consensus, trust scores, emission curves — exists to solve this problem. The mechanics folder gave you the math primitives. This folder explains how they're weaponized against manipulation.

---

## 1. The Core Problem (Revisited)

From Mechanics, the ideal rank score for peer `i` is:

$$r_i \approx \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j}^{n} \sum_{x \in D_j} \Delta F^T(x)_i \cdot H(Q_j(x)) \cdot \Delta F(x)_i$$

This is the **pruning score** — how much the network loses if peer `i` is removed. Theoretically perfect. Practically broken, because:

- The blockchain cannot audit model parameters
- Validators can submit any weights `wᵢⱼ` they want
- A colluding cabal sets internal weights to 1, external to 0, farms TAO without working

The ranking formula alone:

$$R = W^T \cdot S$$

...is trivially gameable. Yuma Consensus is the fix.

---

## 2. Emission Mechanics

### How new TAO enters the system

Every block, a fixed amount of TAO `τ` is minted and distributed across the network:

$$S_{t+1} = S_t + \tau I$$

- `τ` = emission per block (decreases over time via halving schedule — see Token folder)
- `I` = incentive vector — the normalized share each peer receives

### Where emissions go

Each subnet has its own emission pool. The split within a subnet:

|Recipient|Share|
|---|---|
|Subnet Owner|18%|
|Validators|41%|
|Miners|41%|

These are approximate protocol defaults. The key point: **validators and miners share equally at the top level**, so validators are incentivized to find and rank the best miners (their earnings depend on it).

### Emission flows to stake

TAO earned increases your stake `sᵢ`. More stake = more weight in `R = WᵀS`. This creates a **compounding advantage** for early honest performers — they accumulate stake, which amplifies their influence, which lets them direct more rewards toward genuinely good work.

## 3. Yuma Consensus

### What it is

Yuma Consensus is Bittensor's mechanism for aggregating validator weight matrices into a single honest ranking — **resistant to colluding minorities**.

The core insight:

> Validators who agree with the honest majority should earn more. Validators who deviate should earn less. Over time, deviation becomes economically irrational.

### Step-by-step breakdown

#### Step 1 — Validators submit weights

Each validator `i` submits a row of weights `wᵢⱼ` scoring each miner `j`. This forms the weight matrix:

$$W = [w_{i,j}] \quad \text{where } \sum_j w_{i,j} = 1 \text{ for each } i$$

Weights are normalized — each validator distributes 1 unit of score across all miners.

#### Step 2 — Compute weighted median (consensus weights)

For each miner `j`, take the **stake-weighted median** across all validators' scores:

$$\tilde{w}_j = \text{median}_S(w_{*,j})$$

The weighted median is used instead of the mean because **it is robust to outliers and manipulation**. A small group of validators submitting extreme weights (e.g. colluding validators giving their miners 1.0) cannot move the median unless they control >50% of stake.

#### Step 3 — Compute Trust

Trust measures how close a validator's weights are to the consensus:

$$T_i = \sum_j \min(w_{i,j},\ \tilde{w}_j)$$

- If validator `i`'s weights match consensus → `Tᵢ` is high
- If validator `i` deviates (inflates allies, ignores others) → `Tᵢ` is low
- Range: `[0, 1]`

**Trust is the penalty mechanism.** A colluding validator has low trust, which directly reduces their emissions.

#### Step 4 — Compute Rank

Miner rank is the stake-weighted sum of consensus weights:

$$R_j = \sum_i s_i \cdot \tilde{w}_j$$

This is the "cleaned" version of `R = WᵀS` — using consensus weights `W̃` instead of raw submitted weights `W`.

#### Step 5 — Compute Incentive

Miner incentive is normalized rank:

$$I_j = \frac{R_j}{\sum_k R_k}$$

This gives each miner their share of the total emission pool. Sums to 1 across all miners in the subnet.

#### Step 6 — Compute Validator Emission

Validator `i`'s emission is proportional to their **stake × trust**:

$$E_i^{\text{validator}} \propto s_i \cdot T_i$$

This is the key lever: **trust multiplies stake**. A high-stake validator who cheats sees their effective emission weight slashed. An honest low-stake validator earns proportionally more than a dishonest high-stake one.

## 4. The Collusion Attack — Why It Fails Under Yuma

### The attack

A cabal of validators controls stake `S_c`. They:

- Assign weight 1.0 to cabal miners
- Assign weight 0 to all honest miners

### Why it fails

**Weighted median defense:** The consensus weight `w̃ⱼ` for honest miners is determined by the stake-weighted median of _all_ validators. As long as the cabal controls less than 50% of stake, the median is set by honest validators. Cabal miners get low consensus weights regardless of cabal votes.

**Trust penalty:** Cabal validators' submitted weights deviate from consensus. Their trust `Tᵢ` drops toward 0. Their validator emissions collapse.

**Stake erosion:** With near-zero emissions, cabal stake grows slower than honest validators. Over time, their share of total stake `S` shrinks, further reducing their influence on `R`.

### The threshold

The attack only works if the cabal controls **>50% of total stake**. This is the Byzantine fault tolerance boundary — identical to Bitcoin's 51% attack problem. Acquiring >50% of TAO stake to run this attack is prohibitively expensive.

## 5. Trust Score — Deep Dive

$$T_i = \sum_j \min(w_{i,j},\ \tilde{w}_j)$$

Think of this geometrically: trust is the **overlap** between a validator's weight distribution and the consensus distribution.

|Validator behavior|Trust|
|---|---|
|Perfectly matches consensus|`Tᵢ = 1.0`|
|Inflates some miners above consensus|Overshoot ignored, trust reduced|
|Zeroes out some miners others score|Miss penalized, trust reduced|
|Full cabal voting (self-isolated)|`Tᵢ ≈ 0`|

**Key property:** You cannot increase trust by inflating your allies above consensus — the `min()` function caps your contribution at the consensus level. You can only match or fall below it.

---

## 6. Rank vs Incentive vs Emission — What's the Difference?

These terms are related but distinct:

|Term|Formula|What it measures|
|---|---|---|
|**Raw Rank**|`R = WᵀS`|Naive stake-weighted validator votes (gameable)|
|**Consensus Rank**|`R = W̃ᵀS`|Stake-weighted votes using cleaned consensus weights (Yuma output)|
|**Incentive (miners)**|`I = normalize(R)`|Each miner's share of total subnet emission|
|**Emission (validators)**|`E ∝ s · T`|Validator reward weighted by stake × trust|
|**Stake update**|`S(t+1) = S(t) + τI`|How stake evolves over time|

## 7. Bonds — Validator Skin in the Game

Validators don't just vote — they hold **bonds** in the miners they score highly.

$$B_{i,j} = \text{validator } i\text{'s bond in miner } j$$

Bonds work like a financial stake in a miner's success:

- If miner `j` earns high incentive, bonded validators earn a share
- If miner `j` earns low incentive, bonded validators earn less

### Why bonds matter

Bonds create a **long-term alignment** between validators and miners:

- Validators are incentivized to find _genuinely good_ miners early (before others bond with them)
- Validators can't just vote for whoever is popular — they need to correctly predict future performance
- This discourages lazy consensus-following and rewards actual evaluation

Bonds also make collusion more expensive: to profit from fake miners, colluding validators must bond into them, concentrating risk in low-quality assets.

## 8. Incentive Flow Summary

```
Validators submit W (weight matrix)
        ↓
Yuma computes consensus weights W̃ (weighted median)
        ↓
Trust T computed: how close each validator is to W̃
        ↓
Miner Rank: R = W̃ᵀS
Miner Incentive: I = normalize(R)
Validator Emission: E ∝ s · T
        ↓
TAO distributed: S(t+1) = S(t) + τI
        ↓
Honest validators accumulate stake → more influence → system self-reinforces
```

---

## 9. Why the System Is Self-Reinforcing

- Honest validators gain stake → more weight in consensus → harder for cabals to deviate
- High-quality miners gain stake → attract more validator attention → more bonding
- Bad actors lose stake share over time → their influence shrinks passively
- Network converges toward honest majority as dominant equilibrium

This is the **Nash equilibrium** Bittensor is designed around: given everyone else is honest, the most profitable strategy is also to be honest. Defecting only pays if you can acquire >50% of stake first.
