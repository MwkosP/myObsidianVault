


# Bittensor — Participants

---

## Overview

The Bittensor network has three classes of participant. Each is self-interested, permissionless to join, and survives only by performing their role well. There is no employment, no approval process, no safety net — only incentives and competition.

```
Subnet Owner defines the problem
        ↓
Miners compete to solve it
        ↓
Validators judge the solutions
        ↓
Protocol rewards winners in TAO
```

---

## 1. Miners

### What they do

Miners are the **producers of intelligence**. They run models and respond to queries issued by validators. Their output is the raw commodity the network is buying.

### How they earn

- Validators score miner outputs and assign weights `wᵢⱼ`
- Those weights feed into the ranking formula `R = WᵀS`
- Miners with high rank receive a larger share of the emission `τI`
- Poor performers receive little or nothing and eventually exit

### What they compete on

Entirely depends on the subnet. Could be:

- Quality of text generation
- Accuracy of financial predictions
- Speed + quality of image synthesis
- Any other measurable intelligence task

### Key pressure

Miners have **no guaranteed income**. A better miner entering the subnet directly reduces your share. This is the Darwinian pressure that drives quality up over time.

### Stake

Miners can hold stake `sᵢ` but their primary earnings come from **work output**, not stake weight. Stake amplifies influence but doesn't replace performance.

---

## 2. Validators

### What they do

Validators are the **judges of intelligence**. They query miners, evaluate their outputs, and assign weights `wᵢⱼ` — their score of how useful each miner is.

These weights are submitted on-chain and form the weight matrix `W`:

$$W = [w_{i,j}]$$

Where peer `i` sets the `i`-th row — their personal scoring of all other peers.

### How they earn

Validators earn TAO for **ranking honestly** — meaning their weights agree with the consensus of other high-stake validators. Yuma Consensus is what enforces this (see Incentives folder).

### Why honesty is enforced

A validator that submits fake weights (e.g. colluding with specific miners) deviates from the honest majority. Yuma Consensus detects this deviation and reduces their rewards. Over time, dishonest validators get outcompeted and lose stake.

### Stake is everything for validators

Unlike miners who compete on output quality, validators compete on **stake size and ranking accuracy**. More stake = more influence over `R = WᵀS`. This is why validators aggressively accumulate TAO.

### Key pressure

If you rank miners poorly or dishonestly, you earn less than validators who rank accurately. Your stake erodes relative to honest validators, reducing your future influence.

---

## 3. Subnet Owners

### What they do

Subnet owners **define the problem**. They create and maintain a subnet — specifying:

- What task miners must perform
- How outputs are measured and scored
- The rules validators use to rank miners

Think of them as the architects of a competition. They don't mine or validate — they design the game.

### How they earn

Subnet owners receive an **18% cut of their subnet's emissions** automatically. This is protocol-enforced — no negotiation needed.

$$\text{Subnet Owner Emission} = 0.18 \times \tau_{\text{subnet}}$$

### Incentive to attract talent

A subnet owner only earns if their subnet has active, high-quality miners and validators. A dead subnet earns nothing. So subnet owners are incentivized to:

- Define well-scoped, solvable problems
- Make scoring rules clear and fair
- Market their subnet to attract capable miners
- Attract validators with enough stake to matter

### Key pressure

Subnets compete with each other for miner and validator attention. A subnet with unclear goals, low emissions, or unfair scoring will be abandoned. The best subnet owners create environments where the best miners want to work.

## 4. How the Three Interact

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              SUBNET OWNER                   │
│  Defines task, scoring rules, subnet params │
└──────────────────┬──────────────────────────┘
                   │ sets the game
        ┌──────────▼──────────┐
        │       MINERS        │
        │  Produce outputs    │◄──── compete against each other
        └──────────┬──────────┘
                   │ outputs scored by
        ┌──────────▼──────────┐
        │     VALIDATORS      │
        │  Assign weights W   │◄──── compete against each other
        └──────────┬──────────┘
                   │ W fed into
        ┌──────────▼──────────┐
        │   R = WᵀS           │
        │   Yuma Consensus    │
        └──────────┬──────────┘
                   │
        ┌──────────▼──────────┐
        │  TAO distributed    │
        │  S(t+1) = S(t) + τI │
        └─────────────────────┘
```

## 6. Permissionless Entry

Any participant can join any subnet at any time with no permission required:

- A miner just starts responding to validator queries
- A validator registers and starts submitting weights
- A subnet owner pays a registration fee in TAO to create a subnet

The only gatekeeping is **economic** — if you perform poorly, emissions go to others. Bad participants are automatically priced out, not banned.
